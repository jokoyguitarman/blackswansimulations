Gap Report: applied vs. not applied

For the Post-Terrorism Communications Crisis document, the Blueprint Review flagged three Gap items. This walks through each one and shows what the generated scenario and the live experience become if you apply the suggested edit versus if you leave it as-is — based on how the system behaves today.

First, how "applying" works today. The Gap Report is advisory — the AI does not auto-apply a suggestion, and the review screen is read-only. So "applied" here means: you edit the source document and re-upload / re-run extraction. "Not applied" means you proceed with the current extraction as shown. Item 1 — Government & Local Media: internally inconsistent drivers

Emotional Drivers contains "Commitment to accuracy" alongside "Overreact to misinformation" and "Pressure to provide updates before facts are complete" — which can conflict with the stated role "source of factual reporting." Suggested: rephrase or qualify (e.g. "may over-correct / over-emphasise debunking under pressure").

These drivers feed persona generation regardless of the warning — the faction's drivers go into the prompt that creates its accounts. The warning only tells you they're in tension.

If NOT applied (as-is)

The official/media accounts are generated with the contradiction baked in. In the run they behave erratically: sometimes a careful "we won't speculate" voice, sometimes panicky over-correction or publishing before facts. Because this is your designated factual source, trainees may find the official channel is an unreliable anchor — which can be realistic, but can also muddy whether players can trust "official" posts as ground truth.

If applied (edited & re-run)

You qualify the drivers (e.g. "accurate, but under deadline pressure occasionally publishes early and then corrects"). The accounts become a coherent, mostly-reliable authority with realistic but bounded slips. Trainees get a stable factual anchor while still seeing authentic media-under-pressure behavior — the tension becomes intentional rather than random.

Item 2 — Far-Right faction: explicit external link

Behaviour Patterns includes an explicit external link ("Refer to https://archive.4plebs.org/pol/") not integrated into the simulation, possibly inappropriate for a design doc. Suggested: remove or replace with a general description ("fringe forums" / "extremist message boards") + safeguards about not directing participants to it.
Key point: the live output is the SAME either way — because the safety layer enforces it regardless of your edit. The link is never fetched and never posted; far-right agitator content is rendered as recognizable bait with no real links, slogans, orgs, or calls to violence. So this item does not change what trainees see.

If NOT applied (as-is)

The link stays in your document and lands in the catch-all as "tone intent." The containment guardrail drops it, so the generated/live content is safe and unchanged. The only downsides are non-functional: the Gap warning re-appears every run, and your design document itself still contains a real extremist link (a hygiene / shareability concern, not an output one).

If applied (edited & re-run)

You replace it with "fringe forums / extremist message boards." The faction's tone intent is captured generically, the Gap warning disappears, and the document is clean to share. The trainee experience is identical to the as-is case (still safe bait) — the win here is purely document hygiene and removing any chance of the URL ever reaching a prompt.

Item 3 — Time Progression: stages not tied to triggers

Stages are listed but not mapped to time markers; the Escalation Mechanics introduce a separate Low/Medium/High/Critical intensity ladder with approximate windows. Suggested: integrate stage timing criteria so triggers move the scenario from one stage to the next.

Today the runtime advances stages on a time-proportional clock (stageForElapsed spreads your 10 stages evenly across the exercise duration), and the Scenario Director reads the current stage plus the live social_state. The intensity ladder is stored as descriptive context, not a hard transition mechanic.

If NOT applied (as-is)

Stages advance purely on the clock — e.g. "Extremist Exploitation" arrives around the mid-point whether or not players have done anything to provoke it. The Low/Med/High/Critical ladder informs flavor but doesn't gate transitions. Experience: a time-paced arc; escalation tiers are descriptive, and the Director improvises within whatever stage the clock says.

If applied (edited & re-run)

You add explicit "advance when X happens" criteria. This gives the Director richer context to reason over (it can read your transition criteria in the blueprint and time its beats more sensibly), and your document is clearer. Honest caveat: the deterministic stage clock is still time-based today — fully trigger-gated stage transitions (stages that only advance when a condition is met) would need a small additional build. So applying this improves clarity + Director judgment now, but doesn't by itself make transitions strictly event-driven.

Item 3 is the one where "applying" helps but isn't fully realized by current mechanics — it's a good candidate for a future enhancement (trigger-gated stage transitions) if event-driven pacing matters to you.
