1. The two-layer scoring model

Nothing is scored instantly or by simple keyword counting. There are two layers working together:

Layer 1 · per answer

AI content grading

Every time a trainee publishes a post or reply, an LLM grades that single piece of writing against a format-specific rubric. It returns five 0–100 sub-scores (accuracy, tone, cultural sensitivity, persuasiveness, completeness), an overall, written feedback, and 8 true/false "signals".

Source: server/services/contentGraderService.ts → grade stored on social_posts.sop_compliance_score.

Layer 2 · whole session

The deterministic state engine

Roughly every 30 seconds, the engine throws away the old score and recomputes the entire session from scratch: it buckets actions into tiers, ages unanswered hostile posts, averages the AI grades, and derives four gauges plus one headline Overall Sentiment.

Source: computeSocialState() in server/services/socialStateUpdaterService.ts, run by the inject scheduler tick.

The mental model

Layer 1 answers "how good was this specific message?". Layer 2 answers "given everything the team has done and failed to do, how is the crisis trending right now?". The AI grades are inputs into Layer 2, but most of Layer 2 is plain arithmetic over the team's actions and the clock — not over the words.

3. What triggers scoring

Three things move the numbers. Two are events the trainee causes; one is just the clock.

TriggerWhat firesEffect on score
Trainee posts / replies An async AI grade is computed for that post and written to sop_compliance_score. A matching player_action row is recorded. Feeds the next Layer-2 recompute. A grade ≥70 spawns a supportive reaction; a grade <40 spawns a skeptical reaction.
Trainee does an action Flag, report, like, repost, read a news item / email, fact-check, send an email. Each is logged to player_actions with an action type (and sometimes an SOP step). Changes tier counts, the strategic ratio, and "did they do X" flags on the next recompute.
The scheduler tick (~30s) computeSocialState() recomputes the whole scorecard, broadcasts social_state.updated over WebSocket, checks response deadlines, and runs the engagement (virality) tick. This is when the gauges on the trainer dashboard actually change. A missed response deadline applies a flat −10 to sentiment.

Timing matters even when the trainee does nothing. Hostile posts left unattended age into higher penalty tiers, and missing the official-statement clock spawns negative press injects. Standing still is itself a scored choice.

4. Answer types & how each is graded

The grader is given the crisis facts, the harmful post being answered, the hidden best-practice rubric, the elapsed time, and the declared post format. The format decides which rubric the LLM uses, so the same words score differently depending on whether the trainee tagged them as a "text reply", "official statement", or "meme".

The five graded dimensions (every format returns these 0–100)

DimensionThe grader is asking…Feeds Layer-2
AccuracyOnly verified facts? Cites official sources? No speculation?Public Trust
ToneEmpathetic, calm, authoritative — not defensive or preachy?Stakeholder Confidence
Cultural sensitivityAppropriate for context? Avoids amplifying harm? Aware of affected people?Stakeholder Confidence
PersuasivenessWould a worried stakeholder be convinced? Addresses the real fear?Narrative Control
CompletenessActionable guidance? Verified facts? Points to the right channel?(feedback only)

⚠ The anti-amplification rule

If a reply quotes or repeats the harmful content verbatim — even to debunk it — the grader is told to deduct −20 persuasiveness and −10 cultural sensitivity. Repeating the lie to "set it straight" is penalised because it spreads the lie further.

Worked examples — all replying to @WorriedMum_Jo

Text reply · strong
"We hear how frightening this is for any parent. Here are the facts: only batches L2231–L2233 are recalled, for a cap-seal defect — not contamination. No illness has been confirmed. Check your batch code or get a full refund here: puresip.com/check. Our team is here all day."
ACC 90 TONE 88 SENS 85 PERS 82 OVERALL 87

"Leads with empathy, corrects the scope of the recall with a verified fact, and gives a concrete next step. Does not repeat the false 'poison' claim verbatim."

Signals true: acknowledged_affected_parties, includes_actionable_guidance, cites_verified_sources, addresses_specific_claims, avoids_harmful_amplification, promotes_constructive_dialogue

Text reply · weak but safe
"Thanks for reaching out. We take all concerns seriously and are looking into it. Please stay tuned for updates."
ACC 55 TONE 60 SENS 58 PERS 38 OVERALL 52

"Polite but empty corporate-speak. Provides no facts, no scope correction, and no action, so it fails to reassure or to counter the narrative."

Signals true: promotes_constructive_dialogue. Everything else false — no facts, no guidance, no specific claim addressed.

Text reply · harmful
"It is absolutely FALSE that 'PureSip poisoned my baby and we knew and stayed silent.' These accusations are defamatory and we will be contacting our lawyers."
ACC 60 TONE 25 SENS 30 PERS 22 OVERALL 30

"Defensive and legalistic. Worse, it quotes the harmful claim verbatim — triggering the anti-amplification deduction (−20 persuasiveness, −10 sensitivity) — and threatening a scared parent reads as a cover-up."

Signals true: none. An OVERALL < 40 will spawn a skeptical NPC reaction questioning the response.

5. Actions, tiers & the strategic ratio

Every action is sorted into one of three tiers. The engine doesn't care much about how many things you did — it cares about the mix. Frantically replying to everyone (Tier 1) without ever publishing a coordinated response (Tier 2/3) is the losing pattern.

TierAction types (as recorded)Meaning
Tier 1 · Reactive reply_posted, post_liked, post_reposted, post_flagged, news_read Firefighting and monitoring. Necessary, but not strategy.
Tier 2 · Strategic post_created, fact_checked, email_read, draft_created, draft_published Producing your own narrative and verifying before you speak.
Tier 3 · Advanced email_sent, escalated, draft_submitted_for_approval, draft_approved, call_answered Coordination, escalation, stakeholder outreach.

Struck-through types are defined in the tier lists but nothing in the current app records them — see §9. Today only email_sent reliably reaches Tier 3.

strategic_ratio = (tier2 + tier3) / total_actions

  • Ratio > 0.4 (and > 5 actions) → final sentiment × 1.1 bonus.
  • Ratio < 0.2 (and > 5 actions) → final sentiment × 0.85 penalty.

Quirk: passive reads (news_read, email_read) count as actions, so heavy reading inflates the denominator (and email_read even inflates Tier 2).

How "fact-checking" is detected

Fact-checking is worth +3 Public Trust each, a Tier-2 point, and unlocks the fact-check-then-post Narrative bonus — but how the system detects it is looser than it sounds, and the three paths don't score the same way.

Player does…Action recordedSOP fact_check stepPublic Trust +3 & Tier 2?
Opens a "verified facts" email fact_checked ✓ marked ✓ yes
Files a dispute against a false claim dispute_filed ✓ marked ✗ no
That dispute is upheld / rejected by the adjudicator dispute_upheld / dispute_rejected ✓ marked ✗ no

The trigger for the scoring one is simply the player opening an email whose email_category is verified_facts:

// routes/socialMedia.ts  - POST /emails/:emailId/read
if (email.email_category === 'verified_facts') {
  recordPlayerAction(session_id, user.id, 'fact_checked', emailId, null, {}, 'fact_check');
}

⚠ It only checks that they opened the briefing — not that they used the facts. Just reading the email earns the +3. Meanwhile the more sophisticated act of formally disputing a false claim earns no Public Trust and no Tier-2 credit (it only ticks the SOP timeline). See §9.6.

6. The four dimensions + composite

Each recompute rebuilds four 0–100 gauges from scratch, clamps them, then blends them into Overall Sentiment. These are the exact formulas in computeSocialState().

Public Trust baseline 50

publicTrust  = 50
  + (avgAccuracy - 50) / 5          // from AI grades
  + factChecked_count * 3
  - unaddressedMisinfo_count * 2
  - floor(oldestMisinfoMinutes / 5) * 2   // only if oldest > 10 min

Stakeholder Confidence baseline 40

communitySafety = 40
  + (communityLeaderContacted ? 15 : 0)   // an email to a leader/regulator/etc.
  + (avgSensitivity - 50) / 5
  + (avgTone - 50) / 8
  - min(fearPosts, 10) * 1.5               // negative-sentiment NPC top-level posts
  - (rallyCallActive ? 10 : 0)

Narrative Control baseline 20 + reach

impressionRatio = playerViews / hostileViews   (capped at 2.0)

narrativeControl = 20 + min(impressionRatio, 2.0) * 30
  + (officialPublished ? 10 : 0)           // ⚠ see §9 - currently never true
  + (avgPersuasiveness - 50) / 5
  - weightedHatePenalty * 0.5
  + (factCheckThenPost ? 5 : 0)
  + (draftApprovePublish ? 5 : 0)          // ⚠ see §9 - currently never true

Narrative Control is the heaviest gauge in the composite, so its two disconnected bonuses (below) matter a lot.

Escalation Risk baseline 20 · inverted (lower = better)

escalationRisk = 20
  + rallyPosts * 12
  + violencePosts * 8
  - platformReports * 3
  - post_reported_count * 2                // ⚠ double-counts the same reports

The weighted "ignored hostile post" penalty

For each harmful post the team hasn't replied to, flagged, or reported:

weightedHatePenalty += ageTier * authorWeight

ageTier:  <2m=0   2-5m=1   5-10m=2   10-15m=3   >15m=4
weight:   designed agitator (inject / persona / antagonist page) = 3
          ordinary ambient NPC = 1

So ignoring @TruthDropper (a designed NPC) for 11 minutes adds 3 × 3 = 9 to the penalty, dragging Narrative Control down by 4.5.

Overall Sentiment (the headline number)

sentiment = round( 0.20*PublicTrust
                 + 0.20*StakeholderConfidence
                 + 0.40*NarrativeControl
                 + 0.20*(100 - EscalationRisk) )

then:  * 1.1   if strategicRatio > 0.4 and totalActions > 5
       * 0.85  if strategicRatio < 0.2 and totalActions > 5

Colour bands on the dashboard: > 60 30–60 < 30 (Escalation Risk is inverted).

8. Consequence triggers

Beyond the gauges, crossing a threshold spawns a one-shot, in-world reaction (a new NPC post or news item). These fire on the edge — the moment a condition becomes true — not repeatedly.

9. ⚠ Scoring steps that look broken

You asked specifically because recent changes may have invalidated parts of scoring. Tracing the live code against what the engine expects, here is what I found. The headline: the old draft → approve → publish workflow was replaced by direct posting, but the scoring formulas and flags were never rewired to the new path.

9.1 — "Official statement published" never registers high impact

The flag official_statement_published and the Tier-2 actions draft_created / draft_published are wired to action types that nothing in the app records anymore. The new DraftPad just calls POST /social/posts with plain content; the composer marks an official statement only via post_format = 'official_statement' (which sets a different flag, player_posted_official_statement, and the SOP publish step).

Consequences of this single break:

  • Narrative Control loses its +10 "officialPublished" bonus — permanently (worth ~4 pts of Overall Sentiment in the worked example).
  • The trainer's "Official statement published" checklist stays ✗ even after the team posts one — while the expandable list underneath does show the official-format post. Confusing and wrong.
  • The positive press consequence ("a news outlet shares the official statement") never fires.
  • The "no statement at 20 / 35 min" negative consequences keep firing even after a statement is posted.
  • stakeholder_statement_issued (needs leader-contacted and officialPublished) can never be true.

Files: socialStateUpdaterService.ts (officialDrafted/officialPublished lines 308–309, NC +10 at line 375), vs the actual post path in routes/socialMedia.ts (line 447) and DraftPadApp.tsx (publishDraft).

9.2 — The draftApprovePublish +5 bonus is unreachable medium

It requires the SOP steps draft and approve and publish to all complete. No action in the app ever sets sop_step_matched = 'draft' or 'approve', so the SOP draft/approve steps never complete and this +5 to Narrative Control is dead. (The SOP timeline will also always show "Draft" as red/overdue.)

9.3 — Most of Tier 3 is unreachable medium

Of the Tier-3 action types, only email_sent is ever recorded. escalated, draft_submitted_for_approval, draft_approved, and call_answered are defined in the tier list but never emitted, so the strategic ratio's "advanced" bucket is effectively just outbound email. (Escalation still counts via the email, so the impact is limited — but the tier list is now misleading.)

9.4 — Escalation Risk double-counts platform reports low / pre-existing

Each post_reported is subtracted twice — once as platformReports * 3 and again as post_reported_count * 2 — so every report drops Escalation Risk by 5, not the likely-intended 3. Looks like a copy/paste leftover rather than a recent break.

9.5 — Display vs counter mismatches low

  • "Unattended Harmful Posts" / "Live Feed" panels show the top 50 by virality, while the gauge counters use all posts — a low-virality old post can be counted but not listed.
  • "Misinfo flagged" counts every flag, not only misinformation flags.
  • The creative-format windows for infographic / personal_story are wired to the generic creative flag, which is only set for memes/videos — so those windows can't unlock.

9.6 — Fact-check credit rewards the wrong action medium

The Public Trust +3, the Tier-2 point, and the fact-check-then-post bonus all key off the fact_checked action — which is only produced by opening a "verified facts" email (regardless of whether the facts are used). Formally filing a dispute against a false claim records dispute_filed / dispute_upheld instead, which tick the SOP fact_check step but earn zero Public Trust and zero tier credit.

Effect: passively reading a briefing is scored as diligence, while the harder, correct behaviour (challenging misinformation through the dispute system) is not. Likely fix: count dispute_filed/dispute_upheld toward the Public Trust and Tier-2 fact-check credit (or reward an upheld dispute more than an email open). Files: routes/socialMedia.ts (lines 958, 1297), services/contentDisputeService.ts (line 353), socialStateUpdaterService.ts (Public Trust line 348, Tier-2 list).

9.7 — post_reported is never recorded medium

Escalation Risk subtracts for post_reported actions (and the engagement engine suppresses a reported post's reach), but no route ever emits post_reported — the frontend only calls /flag, there is no report endpoint. So the entire "report a post to de-escalate" scoring path is dead, and Escalation Risk can only be lowered indirectly. This is the natural thing to fix alongside the scored-reporting feature in §11. Files: socialStateUpdaterService.ts (lines 310, 387), services/engagementAlgorithmService.ts (lines 190–254).

Suggested priority

Fix 9.1 first — it's the one a trainer will actually notice mid-exercise (the checklist contradicting the feed, and a team that "did everything right" still being told nobody published a statement). The cleanest fix is to make computeSocialState treat a posted official_statement as officialPublished (and emit the consequence) rather than relying on the retired draft_published action. I can implement that if you'd like.

10. Proposed enhancement: reward using the facts

This is a design proposal, not current behaviour. Today, "fact-checking" is credited only when a player opens a "verified facts" email (§9.6), regardless of whether they ever use the facts. A better model: score the player on whether their outgoing communications actually use the case's known-true facts — including a takedown / correction request that cites a confirmed fact to debunk a known-false claim.

Why this fits the existing architecture

The case facts are pre-generated and immutable: the fact_sheet.confirmed_facts (and the false unconfirmed_claims with their truth) are authored during war-room generation and stored on scenarios.initial_state (socialCrisisGeneratorService.ts). The verified-facts emails are pre-written copies of that sheet, delivered on a timer. So there is a stable ground truth to grade against.

Half of this already exists: the post grader already compares posts to confirmed_facts and emits cites_verified_sources / addresses_specific_claims signals (contentGraderService.ts), and the statementWatchdogService.ts already reads all outgoing statements and outbound emails against the fact sheet — but only to penalise errors (spawning challenge posts), never to reward correct fact usage.

The gaps this would close

TodayWith this enhancement
Outbound player emails are not graded at all — POST /emails only records email_sent; the sim_emails.sop_compliance_score column is left empty. Every outgoing comm (post, reply, email, dispute) is checked for correct fact usage and scored.
A "please correct / take down this article, here are the confirmed facts" email earns nothing for being right. A correction / takedown request backed by a true fact earns a positive bonus.
Fact-check credit = "opened the briefing email" (a brittle proxy, §9.6). Fact-check credit = "demonstrably used a verified fact in a communication".

Sketch: a unified "fact-usage" evaluator

One evaluator runs on each outgoing communication and returns a structured result against the pre-generated fact sheet:

{
  confirmed_facts_used:      [factId, ...],   // true facts correctly invoked
  false_claims_repeated:     [claimId, ...],  // known-false claims the player echoed
  correction_requested_for:  [claimId, ...],  // asked to rewrite/take down, citing a true fact
  on_topic:                  true | false
}

Map it to scoring:

  • +N Public Trust per distinct, relevant confirmed fact correctly used — this replaces the email-open proxy in §9.6.
  • Bonus when correction_requested_for is non-empty and backed by a true fact — the "takedown/rewrite request with known-true facts" case, which currently scores nothing.
  • Penalty when false_claims_repeated is non-empty — this is what the watchdog already punishes, so unify the two.

Efficient placement: fold this into the existing statementWatchdogService.ts pass. That LLM call already has the fact sheet and the team's outgoing comms (incl. outbound emails) in one prompt — just extend its output to also report correct fact usage and correction requests, so a single call both rewards and penalises instead of adding a second grader.

Things to design around

  • Gaming / fact-stuffing: reward only distinct, on-topic facts and cap the credit, or players paste all facts into every message to farm Public Trust.
  • Cost & latency: an LLM call per comm. Posts are already graded async; emails would need the same async pattern, or batch via the ~2-minute watchdog tick (cheaper, less immediate).
  • Non-determinism: the rest of the engine is deterministic arithmetic; LLM fact-matching is not. Keep the credit bounded and additive so a flaky judgment can't swing the whole score.
  • Ground-truth quality: the facts are themselves AI-generated at scenario gen, so a takedown citing a "true" fact is only as reliable as that generation — consider a trainer review/override of the fact sheet for high-stakes runs.
  • Intent detection: to specifically reward takedown/rewrite requests, the evaluator must classify the comm's intent, not just keyword-match facts.

Files this would touch: services/statementWatchdogService.ts (extend output), routes/socialMedia.ts (grade outbound emails / disputes), socialStateUpdaterService.ts (Public Trust fact credit, replacing the email-open proxy), contentGraderService.ts (shared fact-comparison logic).

11. Proposed enhancement: scored reporting with a reason

This is a design proposal, not current behaviour. Today flagging is a bare, unvalidated button and "reporting" isn't really wired up. The idea: when a player flags/reports a post, ask them why (the violation category, like a real platform), reward correct reports of genuinely harmful content, and penalise frivolous flagging of legitimate speech that isn't inciting or harmful.

Current behaviour (the gap)

  • Only a flag action exists (POST /posts/:postId/flag) — no reason is captured, and it's the same button for everything.
  • Flagging records post_flagged (Tier 1 + SOP monitor), marks the post "addressed" (removing it from the unattended-hostile penalty), and — if the post carries is_misinformation — also records misinfo_flagged (SOP fact_check).
  • Flagging is unvalidated: flagging a harmless, legitimate post scores exactly like flagging genuine hate or incitement. There is no penalty for false flags.
  • "Reporting" is a dead path: Escalation Risk subtracts for post_reported and the engagement engine suppresses a reported post's reach, but nothing ever records post_reported — so players currently cannot lower Escalation Risk by reporting (see §9.7).

Why this fits the existing architecture

Every post already carries a content_flags object (is_hate_speech, is_misinformation, incites_violence, is_organized_pressure, …) — that is the ground truth for whether a post is reportable. So a chosen violation category can be checked against what the post actually is, with no new ground truth required. (Source: socialStateUpdaterService.ts hasHarmfulFlags().)

Sketch

1. Capture the reason. On flag/report, show categories mapped to the existing taxonomy — Hate speech, Incitement to violence, Misinformation, Organized harassment/pressure, Harmful narrative — plus an optional free-text "what do you think this violates?". Store the category (and text) in the player_action metadata and/or on the social_post_flags row.

2. Score it deterministically against content_flags (no LLM needed for the base case):

CaseGround truthOutcome
Correct report Post has a harmful flag (ideally matching the chosen category) reward emit post_reported so it actually lowers Escalation Risk, count toward "actively moderating," and let it "address" the post.
Frivolous / false report Post has no harmful flags (legitimate speech, criticism, negative sentiment) penalise don't let it address anything or reduce escalation; apply a modest moderation-accuracy ding; optionally fire a backlash inject ("users accuse the brand of censoring critics").

Track report precision = correct ÷ total reports, surface it on the trainer dashboard, and let a low precision (spam-flagging) drive the penalty rather than dinging each flag harshly.

3. Optional LLM adjudication for the gray zone. Where the chosen category doesn't cleanly match the flags, reuse the existing dispute adjudicator pattern (contentDisputeService.ts) — it already takes a player's claim, judges it with an LLM + confidence, and applies an outcome. A "report with reason" is structurally the same thing.

Things to design around

  • Free-speech vs harm boundary: define clearly what's protected (negative sentiment, ordinary criticism, off-topic) vs reportable (the harmful flags). A merely critical post flagged as a violation must count as a false report — that's the core teaching point.
  • Keep the penalty modest: a wrong flag isn't catastrophic in real moderation. Let most of the learning come from feedback + the backlash inject, not a big score hit, so players aren't afraid to act.
  • Anti-gaming: cap credit, de-dupe, and weight by whether the post was genuinely harmful so players can't farm "moderation" points.
  • Fix two bugs in the same pass: actually emit post_reported, and remove the §9.4 double-subtraction so each report is counted once.

Files this would touch: routes/socialMedia.ts (flag/report endpoint + reason), socialStateUpdaterService.ts (report scoring, moderation precision, Escalation Risk), contentDisputeService.ts (reuse adjudication for gray-zone reports), frontend feed apps (SocialFeedApp.tsx / FacebookFeedApp.tsx) for the category picker.

12. Proposed: grading against the crisis-comms best-practice framework

This maps the organisation's "Evaluating Social Media Crisis Communication" framework (six attributes + the High-Value Crisis Evaluation Checklist) onto in-game scoring. Some attributes are already partly measured; the rest need new evaluators. The recommendation is not six separate AIs — it's to extend the two evaluators we already have (the per-post grader and the cross-statement watchdog) and add a small number of focused watchdogs, ideally folded into one consolidated "standards" pass to control cost.

The flagship addition: an RDAP posture watchdog

Attribute 4 is the biggest gap and the highest-value addition because it scores character, not just correctness. A dedicated watchdog classifies the team's posture from their statements + actions and rewards movement up the scale:

LevelDefinitionDetectable signal
ReactiveDenies responsibility; does less than required.Deflection, "no comment", blaming others, legal threats.
DefensiveAdmits but fights it; does the least required.Minimising language, "isolated incident", grudging acknowledgement.
AccommodativeAccepts responsibility; does all that's required.Full apology, corrective action, victim acknowledgement.
ProactiveAnticipates responsibility; does more than required.Compensation offered unprompted, preventive commitments, transparency beyond what was asked.

The watchdog returns a posture level (and the evidence) per scan; the engine maps it to a 0–100 posture meter surfaced on the trainer dashboard, feeding Stakeholder Confidence / Public Trust. Calibrate it against the framework's two prompts — the Universal Law ("would we want every org to act as we are?") and Humanity as an End ("are stakeholders treated as people or as a means to protect profit?").

Prerequisite for Attribute 3: classify the crisis cluster at generation time

Victim-centring can only be graded if the scenario knows its responsibility level. Add a crisis_cluster (victim / accidental / preventable) to scenarios.initial_state during war-room generation. Then the evaluator can reward the right empathy posture — e.g. a full apology + compensation for a preventable crisis, but flag "overly accommodating" behaviour for a victim-cluster crisis where the org isn't at fault.

The recommended watchdog set

Rather than six separate AIs (six times the cost/latency), run one consolidated standards pass on the ~2-minute watchdog tick that already has the facts + full multi-platform timeline in context, returning all of:

  • Transparency score + the existing punitive injects (Attr. 2) — extend current watchdog.
  • Consistency score across channels (Attr. 5) — extend current watchdog.
  • RDAP posture level (Attr. 4) — new output field.
  • Victim-centring / responsibility-match (Attr. 3) — new output field, needs crisis_cluster.

Keep Speed (Attr. 1) deterministic (timestamps, no AI) and Clarity (Attr. 6) inside the existing per-post grader (one more dimension). That's effectively one extended cross-statement watchdog + two small grader/metric tweaks, not six new services.

The consolidated pass, in detail (and where it shouldn't be consolidated)

This is the load-bearing decision, so here is the refined version.

Why one pass

The four AI-judged attributes need the same context the watchdog already assembles — the fact_sheet, the full multi-platform statement timeline, and the outbound emails. That context is the expensive part to build and to send; four separate calls pay for it four times and create four cadences to reconcile. The signals also overlap (a legal-threat statement is both "Reactive" on RDAP and a transparency red flag), so isolating them re-derives the same evidence repeatedly. The operational scaffolding already exists too (2-min tick, cooldown, per-session post cap, previousIssues dedupe, social-media-only gating) — four services means four copies of all of it.

The extended output schema

Keep the existing punitive verdict exactly as-is and bolt on a standards block that is always returned:

{
  // --- existing punitive path, unchanged ---
  "issue_found": true,
  "issue_type": "omission",
  "evidence": "...",
  "challenge_post": "...",
  "persona": "fact_checker",
  "news_articles": [...],
  "npc_reactions": [...],

  // --- new: standards scoring (always returned) ---
  "standards": {
    "transparency":    { "score": 0-100, "verdict": "...", "evidence": "..." },
    "consistency":     { "score": 0-100, "fragmentation": ["X says 5 injured, FB says 3"], "evidence": "..." },
    "rdap":            { "level": "reactive|defensive|accommodative|proactive", "score": 0-100, "evidence": "..." },
    "victim_centring": { "match": "appropriate|under|over", "score": 0-100, "evidence": "..." }
  }
}

The cadence trick that makes it work

Punishing is event-driven and must not spam; scoring is continuous and wants to update every tick. Resolve this by decoupling the two outputs of the same call:

  • The punitive injects stay gated by COOLDOWN_MS + MAX_POSTS_PER_SESSION (no challenge post every 2 minutes).
  • The standards scores are written every scan regardless of cooldown — they're just numbers on the session state, not new posts.

The single call fires on the existing tick; its side effects are throttled independently.

Where it should not be consolidated

  • RDAP is a whole-session property. The watchdog scans new statements since last scan (sinceTimestamp), but posture is a session-level judgment — a team can be "Proactive" overall even if the last two minutes were quiet. Feed RDAP the full timeline, not the delta; this is the one seam where a separate call stays on the table if quality disappoints.
  • Multi-task degradation. One call hunting legal liability and classifying a 4-level posture scale and computing cross-channel consistency tends to do each less well than a focused prompt. Manageable with a strong model + structured schema, but real.
  • One bad JSON kills all five. Today a malformed response just means "no challenge post this tick." Here it would also stall every score — so parse defensively per-field, degrading individually rather than all-or-nothing.
  • Tuning friction. Retuning consistency shouldn't force re-validation of the already-calibrated punitive prompt living in the same file.

The pragmatic shape to ship

One pass, structured as separable sub-evaluations, with two deliberate exceptions:

  • Speed stays deterministic (timestamps — no business in an LLM).
  • Clarity stays in the per-post grader (inherently per-post; the grader already has the post in hand).
  • Transparency + Consistency + Victim-centring run in the consolidated watchdog pass (all "compare statements to facts / to each other," same context, same cadence).
  • RDAP is computed from the full timeline — a fifth field in the same call but with whole-session context, kept splittable.

The principle isn't dogmatically "one AI" — it's don't pay for the shared context four times, and don't conflate the throttled injects with the continuous scores. Consolidation is the default; RDAP's session-level scope is the one place to keep the option to split open.

The High-Value checklist → score mapping

Checklist questionWhere it lands

Things to design around

  • Cost & latency: consolidate into one pass; don't spin up an AI per attribute. The existing watchdog already runs every ~2 min with a cooldown and a per-session cap — reuse those guards.
  • Non-determinism: these are LLM judgments layered onto a deterministic engine — keep each contribution bounded and additive, and show the trainer the evidence behind each posture/transparency call.
  • Don't double-punish: the watchdog currently spawns punitive injects; if it also feeds a transparency score, make sure a single omission isn't penalised twice (once in-world, once in the gauge).
  • Cluster calibration: the "overly accommodating" rule means the correct empathy level is scenario-dependent — the same apology can be right for a preventable crisis and wrong for a victim-cluster one.
  • Trainer transparency: surface new meters (RDAP posture, transparency, consistency, speed) on TrainerSimDashboard.tsx so the framework attributes are visible during the exercise, not just in the AAR.
